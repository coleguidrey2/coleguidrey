Mans Search for Games

March 2026

Now is the slave a free man, now all the stubborn, hostile barriers, which necessity, caprice, or "shameless fashion" has set up between man and man, are broken down. In song and in dance man exhibits himself as a member of a higher community, he has forgotten how to walk and speak, and is on the point of taking a dancing flight into the air. Man is no longer an artist, he has become a work of art.

— Friedrich Nietzsche

The era of labor is behind us and receding further into the past. Labor peaked in the mid-nineteenth century, when an average worker logged 3,000 hours a year. Since then, working time has steadily declined—slowly for more than a century, then more rapidly after 2000 and the start of the digital age. Today, an average worker logs 1,500 hours a year, the figure halved. Yet, this figure understates the decline, for the nature of work itself has transformed, from grueling industrial labor to docile office tasks. Work will transform again with the advent of artificial intelligence and robotics, technologies uniquely suited to replace human labor. This may be the final break that severs us from our Dickensian ancestors. If work no longer organizes life, the question becomes unavoidable: what will man do?

Without work, there is play. This collegiate slogan forms the basis of the utopia imagined by Bernard Suits in The Grasshopper.1 In a world where instrumental actions are unnecessary, the activities that remain are done for their own sake—they become play. Games solve the problem of idleness by giving us obstacles to overcome. We already play games, though fewer than we did in youth when leisure was abundant and responsibilities few. If our liberation from labor returns us to the leisure of youth, perhaps Suits is right: we didn't grow out of games, but were forced to give them up.

Games take many forms, from the subtle social contest of flirting at a speakeasy—an open game—to American Football—a closed game.2 If humanity is freed from labor, the question becomes: What games should man play? Or what games are good for man? Questions like these often stall discussion. It helps to start with examples of bad games for man.

Consider the game of pretend. Its aim is to construct and inhabit imaginative worlds. For children this is a formative game, drawing upon the malleability of the developing mind and cultivating creativity, and narrative thinking. Yet the same activity becomes less suitable in adulthood. Adult life demands engagement with reality, and prolonged immersion in solitary fantasy tends toward escapism, and in extreme cases psychological disorder.

Another unsuitable game is Beer Pong. The goal to throw ping pong balls into beer-filled cups, and binge drink, may be a formative game for the new adult. But its close association with binge drinking and poor sleep makes it detrimental to health.

Here we see a pattern. The bad game is not merely unpleasant, it is bad in a precise sense: its operations have detrimental effects on the life of the player, degrading the capacities that constitute his flourishing. A good game must develop the player rather than diminish or corrode him. The goodness of a game therefore relates to the life-form of the creature playing it. This framework follows the account of goodness developed by Philippa Foot in Natural Goodness.3 What is good for a living thing is determined by the kind of thing it is. The evaluation of plants, animals, and human beings alike depends on their characteristic life-form: we judge them according to how well their natural capacities are realized.

To know what games are good for man, then, we must first know what goodness in man consists in.

Continuing with Aristotle, we find man's function, or ergon: rational activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.4 At first, this idea seems to favor intellectual games: chess, trivia, or mentally taxing board games. These intellectual games are not nothing, but they are incomplete because some virtues, namely courage and temperance, are not intellectual dispositions but are physically trained and practiced. You become courageous by doing courageous things — by facing real fear in the body, repeatedly, until the disposition is inscribed. Virtue, on closer inspection, takes on an irreducibly embodied role. They are so inseparable from action that they cannot be realized any other way.

This doesn't go far enough. The rational mind is not the seat of the self but its epiphenomenon — downstream of bodily, prereflective, processes that are already intelligent. The body is the great reason and the mind is its instrument. Bodily excellence is therefore not supplementary to eudaimonia but inseparable from it.

We must correct some misconceptions about bodily excellence. Talk of physical excellence might produce images of steroidal body builders, or the violent collisions of the punt returner. These are naturally defective in Foot's sense, as they lead to the destruction of the heart, and the head, respectively. What more accurately captures bodily excellence is health — not merely the absence of illness but what Nietzsche calls the Great Health, große Gesundheit: overflowing energy and vitality, a superabundance that does not need to conserve itself but can afford to spend freely.5 This is the spirit of games, in Suit's sense: in great health man seeks unnecessary obstacles to overcome.

The Dionysian spirit is of unbridled energy— unbridled, explosive, the ecstasy of full physical exertion. But Dionysian energy alone is formless, overwhelming, finally self-destructive. The Apollonian spirit is its necessary counterpart: measured discipline, form, technique, the shaping of raw force into something excellent and beautiful.6 Bodily excellence requires both, the vitality of energy and the excellence to shape it and act. Good games are those that demand exactly this: that the player bring full vital force and subject it to form.

Merleau-Ponty shows what this cultivation produces at its peak. The body trained over time, facing real resistance, forced to adapt, formed through genuine habituation. The trained body develops what Merleau-Ponty calls motor intentionality: a form of intelligence that operates prior to reflection.7 The body no longer executes commands from the mind; it understands situations directly. At its fullest development, this becomes the flow state in which virtuous action is simply second nature. There are no longer inhibitions, no longer the gap between intention and execution, no longer the self watching itself perform. There is just action. The rational soul is in harmony with virtues. This is what Aristotle means when he says the person of excellent character acts well without struggle because the character is so fully formed that excellent action is simply what the body does.

Good games, then, are fundamentally physical — balancing the Apollonian and Dionysian, demanding the great health and deepening it, cultivating the embodied intelligence that culminates in flow. Their operations just are the acts of flourishing. In a post-labor world, the highest games will not merely fill our time. These games they will become the very practice of human flourishing itself.
